The struggle of Latino American indigenous migrants to get a life way

Discrimination, laboral abuse, and lack of traduction services may mark their chances of get economic and academic opportunities, activist says.

By Eduardo González | Nov. 15, 2022
Lee en Español

NYC's indigenous latino diasporas are 30% less likely to have access to basic economic conditions, according to a study published by the NYC Health Department.

The investigation shows a relation between the level of English and the family income, something that for Próspero Martínez, a migrant from the Mixe community of Oaxaca, has to do with labor abuse.

"Many times in restaurants or Mexican places they don't pay us the minimum wage, they pay 10, 12 dollars an hour. It’s an abuse".

Próspero Martínez

Mixe Native Inmigrant

However, pursuing a professional career is not an option either, since only 26.7% of the members of indigenous communities have access to university education.

Daniel Kaufman (PhD. CUNY), in his paper "Surveying Indigenous Latin American Languages in NYC," says that this lag is because "they often remain dispersed, invisible and without access to services, including translation”.
